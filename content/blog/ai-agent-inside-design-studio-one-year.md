A year ago I gave an AI agent access to our Linear board, our contractor billing data, and our creative brief pipeline.

I didn't do it as an experiment. I did it because I was drowning.

Thirty people. Four active game titles. A contractor roster that turned over 40% every quarter. And a project management system that was technically functional but required constant human triage to stay honest.

Here's what actually happened.

---

## What I expected vs. what I got

I expected the AI to help me write documents faster. Performance reviews, project plans, contractor briefs — the administrative overhead that eats creative leaders alive.

That part worked, and I underestimated how much cognitive load it was carrying. Not having to stare at a blank document every time I needed to write a brief or a review freed up real mental space.

What I didn't expect: the AI became genuinely useful as a monitoring layer.

Not dashboards. Not reports. Actual pattern detection — the kind of thing I was doing in my head but inconsistently. The AI noticed that a contractor's hours had doubled with no corresponding deliverable increase three weeks before I would have caught it at invoice review. It flagged that a game feature had been "in exploration" for two weeks longer than any comparable feature in our history.

These aren't insights that require intelligence. They require consistency — looking at the same signals every week without fail. Humans are bad at this. The AI is good at it.

---

## What didn't change

The creative decisions. Every meaningful call — is this art direction right, does this mechanic feel good, is this contractor the right fit for this project — still required human judgment.

The AI got better at framing those decisions. Surfacing relevant context, pulling comparable examples from our project history, reminding me of decisions we'd made before and why. But it was never in the loop on the actual call.

The relationship layer also stayed human. Contractors, team members, cross-functional partners — those relationships required real presence, real attention, real care. An AI can draft the follow-up message. It can't replace what happens in the conversation.

---

## The unexpected ROI

Six months in, I started tracking a different metric: how many contractor billing disputes we had per quarter.

Before: 3-4 per quarter. Hours logged against vague deliverables, invoices that didn't match expectations, work that didn't connect to anything we'd asked for.

After: 1 in the last two quarters combined. Not because contractors got better. Because our visibility improved. We caught misalignments at week two instead of week six.

That's the real ROI — not time saved on documents, but money not lost to slow-moving accountability gaps.

---

## What I'd tell someone starting this now

**Don't start with the AI tools. Start with the data you're trying to monitor.** The AI is only as useful as the inputs you give it. If your Linear board is a mess, the AI will surface a mess. If your contractor briefs are vague, the AI will flag the wrong things.

Clean up your inputs first. Then add the intelligence layer.

**Pick one problem, not five.** I started with contractor accountability because that was costing us money. Not with "AI for everything." Once that worked, I expanded. Starting with a specific, measurable problem gave us a clear success signal.

**Expect to teach it your context, repeatedly.** The AI doesn't know your studio's history, your contractors' patterns, or your implicit quality standards without you building that context. That work compounds — but it takes time upfront.
