I didn't find out until invoice review.

Six weeks had passed. 159 hours had been logged. And when I actually opened the Figma file, there was one frame. One. A rough exploration that looked like something you'd produce in an afternoon.

I'm not sharing this to embarrass the contractor. I'm sharing it because this exact situation — or some version of it — happens in almost every contractor-heavy creative studio. And it keeps happening because we've built our trust models backwards.

---

## How it started

We brought the contractor in for a specific project: UI design for a new game feature. Clear brief. Clear scope. Hourly engagement, which we use for exploratory work where the final form isn't fully defined yet.

Week 1: twelve hours logged. The status update said "research and exploration." No deliverable attached. No Figma link. We marked it as expected for week one.

Week 3: the hours accelerated. Thirty hours in five days. No check-in from the contractor. No update in Linear. The pattern was visible in the data — it just wasn't visible to anyone who was looking.

By week 6: 159 hours. One Figma frame.

---

## The real problem isn't the contractor

Here's what I had to accept after this: this wasn't a contractor problem. It was a systems problem.

We had no mechanism to catch output gaps in real time. Our visibility into contractor work happened at two points: kickoff (when we assign the work) and invoice (when we pay for it). Everything in between was a black box.

The contractor wasn't necessarily acting in bad faith. They were working in an environment where nobody was checking whether hours were mapping to deliverables. In that kind of environment, scope naturally expands. "Research and exploration" becomes a permanent state. Progress reports disappear.

Trust without visibility is just hope with a budget.

---

## What we built instead

After this, I spent several months rethinking how we track contractor work. Not with more meetings or more micromanagement — we have 20+ contractors at any given time, and I can't review everyone's output daily. We needed a system.

What we landed on: a lightweight accountability layer that tracks hours logged versus deliverables submitted for every contractor, every week. When the ratio drifts — hours climbing, no new files — it surfaces automatically. We catch it at week two, not week six.

The AI agent that runs inside our studio can spot these patterns across every active engagement simultaneously. It's the same logic that would have flagged our contractor at week 3, not week 6.

---

## Three things I do differently now

**1. Deliverable checkpoints replace status updates.** Instead of asking "how's it going," we ask "what did you make this week?" A link to a file is non-negotiable. A written update with no artifact attached doesn't count.

**2. Hours-to-output ratio is tracked, not just hours.** 30 hours in a week is fine if there's work to show for it. 30 hours with nothing to show triggers a check-in. This sounds obvious. Almost nobody does it.

**3. The first week is the signal.** How a contractor performs in week one predicts the engagement. If week one ends with no deliverable and a vague update, that pattern will repeat. Address it immediately or exit the engagement.

## The uncomfortable truth

Most creative studios catch contractor problems late — at invoice, at launch, or when a PM finally asks where the work is. By then, you've already paid. You're negotiating a refund or writing off the loss.

The cost isn't just the invoice. It's the weeks of lost momentum, the rushed replacement work, and the erosion of trust inside your team when it keeps happening.

The fix isn't hiring better contractors. It's building better systems around the contractors you have.
