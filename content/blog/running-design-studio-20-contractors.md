The first time I managed more than twenty contractors simultaneously, I got it badly wrong.

Not spectacularly wrong — nobody shipped broken games or made national news. But quietly, expensively wrong. Contractors who drifted without clear direction. Invoices that didn't match expectations. A creative brief pipeline that produced inconsistent results because it depended entirely on whether I happened to be available that week.

I've since figured out what actually works. Here it is.

---

## The fundamental model shift

When your team is mostly contractors, the traditional management model breaks. You can't build culture the same way. You can't have the same informal feedback loops. You can't assume institutional knowledge carries forward from project to project.

What you have instead is a transactional relationship that can produce great work — but only if the systems around it are strong enough to compensate for the lack of continuity.

The shift: **invest in your systems the way staff-heavy studios invest in their people.**

Brief templates, onboarding documentation, quality standards, feedback frameworks — these need to be written down, updated, and enforced consistently. For a staff team, these things live partially in the team's shared knowledge. For a contractor team, they have to live in documents.

---

## What I systematized first

**Onboarding.** Every contractor goes through the same process: a kickoff call, a written brief with defined deliverables, and a sample review on their first week's output. Not because I assume they're bad — because this is how I calibrate the engagement before we're deep into it.

The sample review at week one is the highest-leverage thing I do. It surfaces misalignments before they become expensive. A contractor who interprets "clean UI" as flat corporate design when I'm looking for something with warmth and dimension — I find that out in week one, not week four.

**Deliverable cadence.** For most roles, I expect one shippable artifact per week. Not a final deliverable — a piece of work I can actually look at. This keeps output moving and gives me real signal on pace.

**Quality benchmarks.** I maintain a reference library of work that meets our standards — organized by type (UI, concept art, motion, etc.). New contractors get this on day one. It eliminates the most common brief failure: the contractor who thought they were meeting expectations but was aiming at a different target.

---

## The part AI changed

For the first few years, all of this monitoring was manual. I reviewed Linear, checked file submissions, tracked hours to outputs in a spreadsheet.

It worked, but it didn't scale past about twelve to fifteen contractors before something started slipping. At twenty-plus, I was making judgment calls with incomplete information because I couldn't review everything.

Adding an AI monitoring layer changed the math. Not by removing me from the loop — by making sure I was looking at the right signals. The AI handles the consistent pattern-matching across all active engagements. I handle the conversations, the decisions, the creative direction.

The combination works in a way that neither does alone. Pure AI monitoring without creative judgment misses context. Pure human monitoring without AI assistance misses patterns.

---

## The things that still require a human

Relationships. A contractor who feels seen, valued, and fairly compensated delivers better work and refers other good contractors. That doesn't come from a system. It comes from genuine engagement.

Creative judgment. The AI can flag that a contractor's work has drifted from the reference examples. It can't tell you whether the drift is actually better. That's still yours.

Culture setting. Even in a contractor-heavy model, you can build something that feels like a studio — a shared sense of quality, a consistent way of working, standards that people are proud to meet. That requires active, intentional effort from the creative lead. It doesn't happen automatically.
