Twenty-three contractors. Four active game titles. One creative director.

If I checked in with every contractor every week, that would be my job. No design reviews, no stakeholder alignment, no actual creative direction — just contractor management calls, back to back.

So I had to figure out how to stay genuinely informed without being present everywhere.

---

## The problem with most contractor tracking

Most studios track contractor hours. Almost none track contractor outputs.

This is backwards. Hours are a proxy metric. What you actually care about is: is work happening, is it the right work, and is it progressing at a reasonable pace?

A contractor can log 40 hours in a week and produce nothing usable. A contractor can log 15 hours and deliver exactly what you needed. The hours tell you one thing. The deliverables tell you the truth.

The shift I made: I stopped asking "how many hours did you log" and started asking "what did you make."

---

## The accountability system I use

**1. File-based proof of work**

Every status update must include a file link. Not a written description of what was worked on — an actual artifact. Figma frame, sketch, reference doc, rough animation, whatever the work produces. If there's no file, there's no update.

This sounds harsh. It isn't. It took about two weeks for contractors to adjust, and then it became default. The discipline of producing a weekly artifact actually clarifies their own progress.

**2. Hours-to-output ratio monitoring**

I track this by role and by project phase. A UI designer in production phase should be generating multiple frames per week per hour range. An art director in concepting phase has more latitude. The ratios aren't fixed — but large deviations from typical patterns are signals worth investigating.

I don't review this manually for every contractor every week. The AI monitoring layer I built flags anomalies automatically. I look at the flags, not the raw data.

**3. The two-week rule**

If a contractor is in "exploration" or "research" mode for more than two weeks with no shippable artifact, that's a conversation. Not an accusation — a conversation. "Walk me through what you've produced and where you're stuck."

Usually the answer reveals either a scope problem (we didn't give them a clear enough brief) or an engagement problem (they're not the right person for this work). Either way, two weeks is early enough to course-correct. Six weeks isn't.

**4. Brief quality as a prevention layer**

Most accountability problems start at the brief. Vague briefs produce vague work. Specific briefs — with reference examples, defined deliverables, and explicit success criteria — give contractors a target and give you a benchmark.

I rewrote our brief template twice before it actually worked. The key additions: (a) a "definition of done" section that lists the specific files expected at each milestone, and (b) a reference examples section with three examples of work quality we're targeting. Not "make it look good." "Make it look like this."

---

## What this doesn't require

It doesn't require weekly check-in calls. It doesn't require a project manager for every engagement. It doesn't require micromanagement or constant availability.

What it requires is consistency — looking at the same signals on the same cadence, every week, without letting it slip. That's where most studios fail. Not because they don't know what to look for, but because the manual effort of reviewing 20+ contractors' output weekly is unsustainable for a single leader.

The AI layer made this sustainable for me. It handles the consistent monitoring. I handle the judgment calls when something surfaces.
