# Issue 1: The middle ground between trust and surveillance

Here's the tension every creative director faces with contractors:

You need to know who's producing. But you don't want to be the kind of boss who reads timesheets like a cop.

The studios that handle this well have found the middle ground. It isn't a management style — it's a structure.

---

## The problem with pure trust

Pure trust — "I'll look at the work when it's done" — works until it doesn't. And when it doesn't, you're six weeks in with a contractor who logged 159 hours, and you're opening Figma for the first time wondering what happened.

We had this happen. I'm not proud of it, but I'm not embarrassed to say it either — because I've talked to enough creative directors to know this is nearly universal. The invoice review is the first time most studios actually cross-reference hours to deliverables.

By then, you've already paid.

---

## The problem with surveillance

The opposite — hourly check-ins, daily standups with every contractor, reading timesheet entries like a detective — creates a different problem. It signals distrust, slows work down, and makes your studio a place good contractors don't want to be.

The best contractors have options. If your studio feels like a surveillance operation, they'll find somewhere else. The ones who stay are the ones who have nowhere better to go.

Neither extreme works.

---

## The structural fix

What works is a lightweight accountability layer that generates visibility without requiring supervision.

The system we use has three components:

**1. File-based proof of work, weekly.** Every status update requires a file link. Not a written description of what was worked on — an actual artifact. Figma frame, sketch, reference, rough animation. If there's no file, there's no update. This one rule eliminates the "I was exploring concepts" problem.

**2. Hours-to-output comparison, automated.** Every Friday, a query pulls hours logged versus assets shipped for each active contractor. The AI does the comparison. It flags anomalies. I look at the flags, not the raw data. One contractor logging 30 hours with 4 assets shipped when their typical rate is 12 assets gets a conversation — not an accusation.

"The AI does the comparison. You do the conversation."

**3. The two-week rule.** If a contractor is in exploration or research mode for more than two weeks with no shippable artifact, that's a structured check-in. We walk through what they've produced and where they're stuck. Most of the time, the answer reveals a brief problem on our side (we weren't specific enough) or a fit problem (this isn't the right person for this work). Either is better to know at week two than week six.

---

## What this changed

In the year before we implemented this system: three to four contractor billing disputes per quarter. Hours billed against vague deliverables, invoices that didn't match what we asked for, work that couldn't be traced to a specific request.

In the twelve months after: one dispute total.

Not because our contractors got better. Because our visibility improved, and because contractors who knew deliverables were being tracked against hours produced — predictably — tracked deliverables against hours.

The structure creates the behavior.

---

## The brief is where accountability starts

The hours-to-output system only works if the brief is specific enough to define what "output" means.

The brief template I use has one section I didn't have before: **Success Criteria** — three to five specific, checkable items that define what done looks like. Not "looks good" or "matches the design language." "Matches the color palette in the token library. Works at 1920×1080 and mobile scales. Approved by lead designer before handoff."

When "done" is defined in the brief, the hours-to-output comparison has a reference point. Without that, you're comparing hours to vibes.
